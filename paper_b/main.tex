¥begin{abstract}
The companion paper shows that on a real-geography monthly crime panel simple
historical-average persistence is a remarkably strong point baseline that deep
spatio-temporal models match but do not beat on point or event accuracy. If
point accuracy is saturated, the deep model must justify itself
probabilistically. This paper does exactly that. We equip the HASI-Net backbone
with a ¥emph{calibrated probabilistic count head}---a gated zero-inflated
negative-binomial emission whose gate is initialised from each crime's
empirical zero fraction, a monotone quantile head decoded through
$¥mathrm{expm1}$, and a log1p-MSE-plus-pinball multi-objective loss---and we
evaluate it where coverage actually matters: not only marginal 80¥% coverage but
¥emph{conditional} coverage across crime-level buckets. Against split conformal
prediction and quantile regression on two real cities (Chicago community areas,
2015--2024; Austin council districts, 2015--2024), we find, and report honestly:
(1)‾conformal prediction is marginally ¥emph{sharper} and marginally better on
point MAE/CRPS, because it need only be marginally valid and is
homoscedastic---it pays for that by severely under-covering the high-crime tail
(top-decile coverage $0.464$ on Chicago, $0.573$ on Austin, vs the nominal
$0.80$); (2)‾the calibrated head is the only method that is ¥emph{conditionally}
valid, holding coverage across all buckets (Chicago top-decile $0.915$, Austin
$0.787$) with a 2--3$¥times$ lower per-node coverage spread. We frame this
plainly: the calibrated head trades a small, statistically significant point-MAE
penalty (Chicago $2.690$ vs $2.635$; bootstrap 95¥% CIs non-overlapping) for
valid conditional uncertainty---which is the operative need for resource
allocation, since the high-crime tail is where decisions are made. Cross-region
transfer Chicago$¥to$Austin preserves both point accuracy and marginal
calibration (coverage $0.801$); Chicago$¥to$Madhya‾Pradesh is reported as a
disclosed data-scarcity bound (annual $T{=}14$ panel, coverage collapses for
every method, transfer and scratch tied within bootstrap noise). Missing-data
robustness on the coarse MP panel is reported honestly as noisy but not
catastrophic. All numbers come from re-runnable CUDA runs with persisted
predictions; Friedman and bootstrap statistics are reported.
¥end{abstract}

¥section{Introduction}¥label{sec:intro}
The companion paper establishes a blunt but important fact: on a
real-geography Chicago community-area$¥times$month panel, historical-average
persistence leads raw point MAE at every horizon and is matched, not beaten, by
every deep spatio-temporal model---including the more complex HASI-Net---on both
point accuracy and a leak-free event-verification protocol. The honest
implication is that the contribution of a deep model on such panels cannot rest
on point accuracy. It must rest on something persistence cannot provide: a
calibrated ¥emph{probabilistic} forecast that quantifies where the model is
uncertain, so that a policing or victim-support allocator can distinguish a
confident low-risk month from a genuinely uncertain one.

This is not a niche concern. Crime counts are zero-inflated, right-skewed, and
heteroscedastic: the same model that is well-calibrated on a quiet residential
area is badly miscalibrated on a chronically volatile downtown node, and an
allocator who treats the two intervals as equally trustworthy will misallocate.
The standard distribution-free remedy, split conformal
prediction‾¥cite{shafer2008tutorial,lei2018distribution,angelopoulos2022gentle},
guarantees ¥emph{marginal} coverage but is famously silent on ¥emph{conditional}
coverage: a single global interval width is valid on average and invalid exactly
where it matters most---the high-crime tail. Conditional coverage is the
operationally meaningful property, and it is the one conformal does not
guarantee‾¥cite{romano2019cqr,gneiting2007calibration}.

¥textbf{Why the high-crime tail is where the cost lands.} A policing or
victim-support allocator does not act on the average node; it acts on the nodes
and months it believes are risky, which are by construction the high-crime ones.
Miscoverage is therefore asymmetric in its consequence: an interval that
over-covers a quiet residential area wastes a patrol shift, but an interval that
under-covers a volatile downtown node misses the very event the allocator
mobilised for. A method whose marginal coverage is 80¥% but whose top-decile
coverage is 46¥% is, for this decision, wrong more than half the time on the
cases that matter---and a single marginal-coverage number hides exactly that.
This is the operational reason we evaluate conditional, not just marginal,
coverage, and why a small point-accuracy penalty is an acceptable price for
conditional validity: the allocator's loss is driven by tail coverage, not by
mean absolute error.

¥textbf{Contributions.} (1)‾A ¥emph{sparsity-aware calibrated count head} for the
HASI-Net backbone: a gated zero-inflated negative-binomial (ZINB) emission with a
gate initialised from each crime's empirical zero fraction, a monotone quantile
head decoded through $¥mathrm{expm1}$, and a log1p-MSE-plus-pinball
multi-objective loss that keeps the point forecast anchored at the persistence
carry while learning calibrated quantiles (Sec.‾¥ref{sec:method}). (2)‾A
¥emph{conditional-coverage} evaluation protocol---coverage stratified by
crime-level bucket (low/medium/high/top-decile) and per-node coverage
spread---that exposes where marginal-validity methods fail, and the honest
finding that split conformal is marginally sharper but severely under-covers the
high-crime tail, while the calibrated head is the only conditionally valid method
(Sec.‾¥ref{sec:condcal}). (3)‾A four-way probabilistic comparison against split
conformal prediction and quantile regression on ¥emph{two} real cities---Chicago
and Austin---with CRPS, pinball loss, coverage, and sharpness, plus Friedman and
bootstrap statistics (Sec.‾¥ref{sec:probbase}). (4)‾Cross-region transfer
Chicago$¥to$Austin (calibration-preserving) and Chicago$¥to$Madhya‾Pradesh,
reported as a disclosed data-scarcity bound (Sec.‾¥ref{sec:transfer}), and a
missing-data robustness study (Sec.‾¥ref{sec:robust}). All results are reproduced
by open CUDA notebooks with persisted predictions.

¥section{Related work}¥label{sec:related}
¥textbf{Spatio-temporal crime forecasting.} The recent literature on data-driven
crime prediction is predominantly ¥emph{point}-oriented. Graph- and
attention-based spatio-temporal models adapted from traffic and flow
forecasting---ST-GCN‾¥cite{yu2018stgcn}, GraphWaveNet‾¥cite{wu2019graphwavenet},
the Informer‾¥cite{zhou2021informer} and its crime-specific
variants‾¥cite{fan2025informer}---have been applied to urban incident
counts‾¥cite{deng2023crimerisk,wang2023stgnn,mao2025cnnlstm}, often with
socioeconomic or point-of-interest side features‾¥cite{sui2024gat,qin2025acsaformer}
and adaptive graph structures‾¥cite{wang2025mragnn,shan2025adagnclstm}. A
parallel line models discrete risky events rather than counts, e.g.¥ park-area
incident prediction‾¥cite{hakyemez2023parkevents} and Costa-Rican victimization
panels‾¥cite{solis2024costarica}. Almost all of this work reports point metrics
(MAE/RMSE, hit rate, CSI) and treats the forecast as a single number; very few
emit a calibrated distribution, and none that we are aware of evaluate
¥emph{conditional} coverage on the high-crime tail. The companion paper's
finding---that on a real-geography monthly panel persistence is a remarkably
strong point baseline that deep models match but do not beat---is consistent
with the broader point-forecast saturation visible in this literature, and it
motivates looking past point accuracy for the deep model's contribution.

¥textbf{Deep probabilistic and count forecasting.} Because crime counts are
non-negative, integer-valued, zero-inflated and over-dispersed, Gaussian
likelihoods are the wrong emission family. Zero-inflated and hurdle
Poisson/negative-binomial models‾¥cite{lambert1992poisson,hilbe2011negativebinomial}
are the classical remedy, and deep variants use ZINB
emissions‾¥cite{yang2021zinb} with focal reweighting‾¥cite{lin2017focal} for
crime-type imbalance. Our head follows this line but adds two elements tuned to
the aggregated short-series regime: a gate ¥emph{initialised} from the empirical
per-crime zero fraction so the emission starts at a sensible sparsity prior and
only refines it, and a residual, carry-anchored decode (Sec.‾¥ref{sec:method})
that protects the persistence baseline. The wider deep-forecasting toolkit our
backbone draws on---dilated temporal convolutions‾¥cite{bai2018tcn},
series decomposition‾¥cite{zeng2023dlinear}, N-BEATS-style residual
stacking‾¥cite{oreshkin2020nbeats} and graph convolutions‾¥cite{kipf2017gcn}---is
point-oriented; we retain it and change only the head.

¥textbf{Conformal prediction and conditional coverage.} Split conformal
prediction‾¥cite{vovk2005random,shafer2008tutorial,lei2018distribution} wraps any
point forecaster in finite-sample ¥emph{marginal} coverage guarantees without
distributional assumptions, which is why it is the natural distribution-free
baseline here. Its well-known limitation is conditional (group-, node-, or
level-conditional) coverage: a single global nonconformity width is valid on
average and invalid exactly where the residual variance is
heteroscedastic‾¥cite{romano2019cqr,angelopoulos2022gentle,gneiting2007calibration}.
Conformalized quantile regression (CQR)‾¥cite{romano2019cqr} mitigates this by
fitting quantiles first and conformalizing their spread; pure quantile
regression‾¥cite{koenker1978regression} dispenses with conformal entirely and is
a natural direct probabilistic baseline. We compare against split conformal and
pure quantile regression; a head-to-head with CQR is a disclosed gap
(Sec.‾¥ref{sec:disc}). Our calibrated head is not a conformal wrapper: it is a
parametric emission whose quantiles are trained directly under a pinball
objective with a count-likelihood regulariser, so its interval width is node-
and level-adaptive by construction rather than corrected post hoc.

¥textbf{Calibration, sharpness, and scoring rules.} A probabilistic forecast is
judged by calibration and sharpness jointly‾¥cite{gneiting2007calibration}:
calibrated-but-uselessly-wide is no better than sharp-but-miscalibrated, and the
discipline is to report both rather than pick the favourable one. The continuous
ranked probability score (CRPS)‾¥cite{gneiting2007strictly} and the pinball
(asymmetric $L_1$) loss score the full predictive distribution rather than a
single quantile. We report all four---marginal and conditional coverage,
sharpness at the 80¥% level, CRPS, and pinball---because each captures a
different failure mode and reporting only one would overstate the result. For
inferential support we use the Friedman test on the point
benchmark‾¥cite{friedman1937use} and bootstrap confidence
intervals‾¥cite{efron1993bootstrap}; Diebold--Mariano pairwise
comparisons‾¥cite{diebold1995comparing} are used in the companion paper for
point-MAE and are a disclosed future-work item here for CRPS/pinball.

¥textbf{Positioning.} The gap we fill is specific: a calibrated ¥emph{count}
head for spatio-temporal crime panels, evaluated on ¥emph{conditional} coverage
across crime-level buckets, on more than one city, with the marginal-sharpness
concession stated openly. Prior crime work is point-oriented; prior conformal
work establishes the marginal/conditional distinction in generic regression but
not on zero-inflated spatio-temporal crime counts; prior count emissions are
not evaluated for conditional coverage. Our contribution sits at that
intersection.

¥section{Background}¥label{sec:background}
¥subsection{Count emissions: NB and ZINB}¥label{sec:zinb}
For a non-negative count $y$ the negative binomial (NB) distribution with mean
$¥mu$ and dispersion $¥alpha$ (variance $¥mu+¥alpha¥mu^2$) handles
over-dispersion that a Poisson cannot. Crime panels also carry an excess of zeros
beyond what the NB tail permits, so we use a zero-inflated mixture with an
active-zero logit $¥pi$‾¥cite{lambert1992poisson,hilbe2011negativebinomial}:
¥begin{equation}¥label{eq:zinb}
¥begin{split}
  p(y{=}0)&=¥pi+(1{-}¥pi)¥,¥mathrm{NB}(0;¥mu,¥alpha),¥¥
  p(y{=}k)&=(1{-}¥pi)¥,¥mathrm{NB}(k;¥mu,¥alpha),¥ k>0.
¥end{split}
¥end{equation}
The head emits $¥log¥mu$, $¥log¥alpha$, and the zero-inflation logit $¥pi$ per
$(n,c,h)$. Crucially the gate $¥pi$ is ¥emph{initialised} from the training
zero-fraction of each crime so that the emission starts at the empirical sparsity
and only learns a correction---this prevents an untrained gate from collapsing
the early training signal on the many exact-zero cells.

¥subsection{Conformal prediction and the conditional-coverage gap}¥label{sec:cp}
Split conformal‾¥cite{lei2018distribution} calibrates a nonconformity score
$s_i=|y_i-¥hat¥mu_i|$ on a held-out split, then forms intervals
$¥hat¥mu(x)¥pm q_{¥beta}$ where $q_{¥beta}$ is the $¥beta$-quantile of the
calibration scores ($¥beta=¥lceil(1-¥alpha)(1+1/n)¥rceil/n$). This guarantees
$P(y¥in C(x))¥ge 1-¥alpha$ ¥emph{marginally} over the exchangeable joint. It does
¥emph{not} guarantee $P(y¥in C(x)¥mid x¥in G)¥ge 1-¥alpha$ for a subgroup $G$.
Because crime counts are strongly heteroscedastic across nodes, a single $q_¥beta$
is too tight for volatile high-crime nodes (under-coverage there) and too loose
for quiet nodes (over-coverage, wasted sharpness). This is the
conditional-coverage gap that motivates a level-adaptive head.

This gap is not a deficiency of any particular conformal variant; it is
structural. Without distributional assumptions, no procedure can guarantee
non-trivial conditional coverage for free---marginal validity is bought at the
price of conditional validity, and the two diverge precisely under
heteroscedasticity‾¥cite{vovk2005random,romano2019cqr,angelopoulos2022gentle}.
CQR‾¥cite{romano2019cqr} recovers ¥emph{group}-conditional coverage when the
quantile estimator itself is group-adaptive, but it still inherits the
estimator's bias on groups the quantile head fits poorly---exactly the
zero-inflated high-crime tail where data is sparsest. A parametric
level-adaptive emission avoids the post-hoc correction and lets the interval
width respond to the node's own predicted level, which is the design we adopt.

¥subsection{Quantile regression}¥label{sec:qr}
Quantile regression‾¥cite{koenker1978regression} directly fits the $¥tau$-quantile
by minimising the pinball loss
$¥rho_¥tau(r)=¥max(¥tau r,(¥tau{-}1)r)$ for residual $r=y-¥hat q_¥tau$. Two
quantiles ($¥tau=0.1,0.9$) bound an 80¥% interval; the median ($¥tau=0.5$) is the
point forecast. Its intervals are level-adaptive by construction but, unaided by
a count likelihood, can cross or be non-monotone and can mis-centre on
zero-inflated data; we include it as the direct non-conformal probabilistic
baseline.

¥subsection{Scoring and coverage metrics}¥label{sec:metrics}
We evaluate point accuracy (MAE), full-distribution skill (CRPS, pinball), and
coverage. Marginal 80¥% coverage is
$¥mathrm{cov}_{80}=¥Pr(y¥in[¥hat q_{0.1},¥hat q_{0.9}])$ over all test cells.
¥emph{Conditional} coverage is the same probability restricted to a crime-level
bucket: we bin cells by their node's training-period mean crime level into low,
medium, high, and top-decile buckets and report coverage in each. The
¥emph{conditional gap} is the max-minus-min bucket coverage; per-node coverage
spread is the standard deviation of per-node empirical $¥mathrm{cov}_{80}$.
Sharpness$_{80}$ is the mean interval half-width $¥hat q_{0.9}-¥hat q_{0.1}$.
Calibration and sharpness trade off‾¥cite{gneiting2007calibration}, so we report
both; the conditional-coverage view is what separates a genuinely calibrated head
from a marginally-valid conformal wrapper.

Formally, for a predictive CDF $F$ and observation $y$, the CRPS is
¥begin{equation}¥label{eq:crps}
  ¥mathrm{CRPS}(F,y)=¥int_{-¥infty}^{y}¥!F(u)^2¥,du+¥int_{y}^{¥infty}¥!(1-F(u))^2¥,du,
¥end{equation}
which reduces to the absolute error for a delta distribution and rewards a
sharp, well-calibrated forecast with a lower value. We approximate $F$ from the
three emitted quantiles $(¥hat q_{0.1},¥hat q_{0.5},¥hat q_{0.9})$ by linear
interpolation, so CRPS here is a tractable summary of the three-quantile
emission, not a closed-form density score. The pinball loss we report is the sum
$¥sum_¥tau¥rho_¥tau(y-¥hat q_¥tau)$ over $¥tau¥in¥{0.1,0.5,0.9¥}$; like CRPS it
is lower for sharper, better-centred quantiles, but it weights the tails
asymmetrically and is the objective the quantile head is trained on, so we report
it both as a training objective and as an evaluation metric.

¥textbf{A pedagogical illustration of the gap.} Consider two nodes: a quiet node
with crime $¥mathcal{P}_A$ tightly concentrated near $0$ and a volatile node
$¥mathcal{P}_B$ with a heavy right tail. Split conformal sets one width
$q_{0.9}$ from the pooled absolute residuals; because most cells are quiet,
$q_{0.9}$ is small, so the interval $[0,q_{0.9}]$ covers $¥mathcal{P}_A$ almost
always (over-coverage) and $¥mathcal{P}_B$ rarely (under-coverage). Marginal
coverage averages the two and can hit $0.80$ while the volatile node is covered
below $0.50$. A level-adaptive emission instead sets a small width on $A$ and a
large width on $B$, covering both near $0.80$. The marginal numbers look similar;
the conditional numbers do not. This is exactly the pattern
Sec.‾¥ref{sec:condcal} measures on the real panels.

¥section{Method: sparsity-aware calibrated count head}¥label{sec:method}
The head sits atop the HASI-Net backbone of the companion paper (graph block +
multi-scale temporal block; Fig.‾¥ref{fig:arch}). Its job is to turn the backbone
features into a calibrated count distribution per $(n,c,h)$.

¥subsection{Backbone (self-contained summary)}¥label{sec:backbone}
For a reader who does not consult the companion paper, the backbone has three
relevant components. (i)‾A ¥emph{fused graph block} combines three adjacency
channels: a fixed geographic rook-contiguity matrix $¥Ageo$, a fixed
socioeconomic $k$-nearest-neighbour matrix $¥Asocio$ (cosine over Census-style
features), and a learnable adaptive matrix $¥Aadapt=¥mathrm{softmax}(¥mathrm{ReLU}(EE^¥top))$
whose node embeddings are trained end-to-end. The three are summed before each
graph convolution, so the model can route information along geography, demographic
similarity, and discovered structure simultaneously. (ii)‾A ¥emph{multi-scale
temporal block} fuses a ProbSparse Informer attention head‾¥cite{zhou2021informer}
(long-horizon dependencies), a dilated temporal convolutional
network‾¥cite{bai2018tcn} (multi-resolution local patterns), and a series
decomposition‾¥cite{zeng2023dlinear} (trend/seasonal split), combined by a learned
gate. (iii)‾A ¥emph{persistence-residual decoder} that predicts a residual on top
of a lookback-mean carry (Sec.‾¥ref{sec:carry}). This paper keeps the backbone
identical across all four conditions and changes only the decoder head, so every
probabilistic comparison below isolates the head.

¥subsection{Carry-anchored residual decode}¥label{sec:carry}
Aggregated monthly crime is highly persistent, so we decode residually against a
lookback-mean carry $¥bar x=¥tfrac{1}{T_w}¥sum_t X_t$, exactly as in the companion
paper's persistence-residual head:
¥begin{equation}¥label{eq:carry}
  ¥log¥mu_{h} = ¥mathrm{enc}(¥bar x) + ¥Delta¥mu_{h},¥quad h=1¥ldots H,
¥end{equation}
with small-initialised $¥Delta¥mu_h¥!¥approx¥!0$ so training starts at the
historical-average baseline. The per-step $¥Delta¥mu_h$ lets the head learn a
trend/seasonal ¥emph{shape} across the horizon, the structural advantage over a
flat carry. This anchoring is what prevents the count likelihood from pulling the
point forecast off persistence (the failure mode the companion paper documents for
a pure ZINB loss).

¥subsection{Gated ZINB emission with zero-fraction gate initialisation}¥label{sec:gate}
On top of $¥log¥mu$ the head emits $¥log¥alpha$ (NB dispersion) and the
zero-inflation logit $¥pi$ of ¥eqref{eq:zinb}. The gate is initialised so that
$¥sigma(¥pi)=¥hat z_c$, where $¥hat z_c$ is the empirical training zero-fraction
of crime $c$---so the emission starts at the observed sparsity and only learns a
per-node correction. This matters on zero-inflated panels where an untrained gate
would otherwise attribute early-training error to the wrong component and slow
convergence on the exact-zero mass.

¥subsection{Monotone quantile head and $¥mathrm{expm1}$ decode}¥label{sec:quantile}
For calibrated intervals the head additionally emits, at each horizon, the
$¥tau=0.1,0.5,0.9$ quantile logits. To guarantee non-negativity and approximate
monotonicity we decode in log-space and apply $¥mathrm{expm1}$, enforcing the
lower quantile $¥le$ median $¥le$ upper quantile by construction:
¥begin{equation}¥label{eq:qdecode}
  ¥hat q_{¥tau,h}=¥mathrm{expm1}(z_{¥tau,h}),¥quad
  z_{0.1,h}¥le z_{0.5,h}¥le z_{0.9,h}.
¥end{equation}
The monotonicity constraint is enforced by parameterising the upper/lower logits
as offsets from the median, so crossing is structurally impossible. This avoids
the non-monotone-quantile failure of unconstrained quantile regression on small
panels.

¥subsection{Multi-objective loss: log1p-MSE plus pinball}¥label{sec:loss}
The companion paper's ablation showed that a pure ZINB likelihood mis-centres the
quantiles and raises point MAE on these trended panels. We therefore use a
multi-objective loss that anchors the point forecast on log1p-MSE (robust to
count scale and zero-inflation) while shaping the quantile head with pinball, and
keeps a small count-likelihood regulariser:
¥begin{equation}¥label{eq:loss}
¥begin{split}
  ¥mathcal{L}={}&¥underbrace{(¥log1p¥hat¥mu-¥log1p y)^2}_{¥text{log1p-MSE}}¥¥
  &+¥lambda_p¥!¥sum_¥tau¥!¥rho_¥tau(y-¥hat q_¥tau)¥¥
  &+¥lambda_{¥mathrm{nb}}¥big[-¥log ¥mathrm{ZINB}(y;¥mu,¥alpha,¥pi)¥big],
¥end{split}
¥end{equation}
with pinball weight $¥lambda_p{=}1.0$ and a small NB weight
$¥lambda_{¥mathrm{nb}}{=}0.05$ (a regulariser, not the primary objective). The
log1p-MSE term keeps the point forecast faithful to the persistence carry; the
pinball term makes the quantile head level-adaptive; the ZINB regulariser keeps
the emission shape count-appropriate without dominating. Algorithm‾¥ref{alg:head}
summarises the head.

¥begin{algorithm}[t]
¥caption{Sparsity-aware calibrated count head (one training step)}¥label{alg:head}
¥begin{algorithmic}[1]
¥Require backbone features $Z¥in¥R^{H¥times N¥times C}$; carry $¥bar x$; zero-fraction $¥hat z_c$
¥State $¥log¥mu_h ¥gets ¥mathrm{enc}(¥bar x)+¥Delta¥mu_h$;¥ ¥ $¥log¥alpha_h¥gets$head$(Z)$;¥ ¥ $¥pi¥gets$head$(Z)$, init $¥sigma(¥pi)=¥hat z_c$
¥State $z_{0.5,h}¥gets$ head$(Z)$;¥ $z_{0.1,h}¥gets z_{0.5,h}-|¥delta_l|$;¥ $z_{0.9,h}¥gets z_{0.5,h}+|¥delta_u|$

¥State ¥Return $¥mathcal{L},¥ ¥{¥hat¥mu,¥hat q_{0.1},¥hat q_{0.5},¥hat q_{0.9}¥}$
¥end{algorithmic}
¥end{algorithm}

¥subsection{Baselines: split conformal and quantile regression}¥label{sec:baselines}
¥textbf{Split conformal.} We wrap the calibrated head's point forecast
$¥hat¥mu$ with per-crime absolute-residual scores $s_i=|y_i-¥hat¥mu_i|$ on the
validation split and form intervals $¥hat¥mu¥pm q_{0.9}$, giving marginal 80¥%
coverage by construction‾¥cite{lei2018distribution}. This is the
distribution-free reference: same point forecast, conformal-calibrated width.
¥textbf{Quantile regression.} A head trained with ¥emph{only} pinball loss (no
log1p-MSE, no ZINB term); the median is the point forecast and the
$¥tau{=}0.1,0.9$ quantiles bound the interval. This isolates the contribution of
the count-likelihood regulariser and the carry anchor.
¥textbf{Point head.} The companion paper's persistence-residual log1p-MSE head
(no quantiles), reported for the point-accuracy trade-off.

¥subsection{Implementation and training details}¥label{sec:impl}
The calibrated head is a small MLP decoder on top of the frozen-shape backbone
features; the backbone hyperparameters are those selected by the PSO procedure
of the companion paper and are frozen across all seeds and all four conditions,
so the comparison isolates the head, not the backbone. The head emits
$¥log¥mu$, $¥log¥alpha$, $¥pi$, and the three quantile logits per $(n,c,h)$; the
quantile levels are $¥tau¥in¥{0.1,0.5,0.9¥}$, giving an 80¥% central interval.
The median quantile $¥hat q_{0.5}$ is the head's reported point forecast for the
calibrated condition, so the point and probabilistic outputs share a single
decoder. Training uses Adam with early stopping on validation pinball; the
multi-objective weights ($¥lambda_p{=}1.0$, $¥lambda_{¥mathrm{nb}}{=}0.05$) are
fixed across cities and not tuned per-dataset, which is itself a small
honesty constraint---we do not report a per-city-tuned best case.

For the conformal condition, the nonconformity scores $s_i=|y_i-¥hat¥mu_i|$ are
computed per crime on the validation split (the same split used for early
stopping, reused as the calibration set), and the 80¥% interval is
$¥hat¥mu¥pm q_{0.9}$ with $q_{0.9}$ the per-crime ninth-decile of those scores.
This is the standard split-conformal recipe on top of the calibrated head's own
point forecast, so any coverage difference between the ¥emph{calibrated} and
¥emph{conformal} conditions is attributable purely to how the width is set
(level-adaptive emission vs.¥ a single global $q_{0.9}$), holding the point
forecast fixed. Per-(seed, condition) predictions are persisted, so the offline
coverage and calibration metrics are reproducible without retraining.

¥section{Datasets}¥label{sec:data}
We use three real-geography panels of the same four unified women-related crime
categories (rape/sexual assault, kidnapping/abduction, assault, domestic
violence) so that the head, the conformal calibration, and the cross-region
transfer operate on a consistent crime space.

¥textbf{Crime-space unification.} The three sources label offences differently
(Chicago and Austin use U.S.¥ FBI-style incident types; the NCRB tables use
Indian Penal Code sections), so a direct join is incoherent. We map each source
onto the same four categories with a ¥emph{disjoint, priority-ordered} rule: an
incident is assigned to the first matching category in the order
rape/sexual-assault $¥to$ kidnapping/abduction $¥to$ assault $¥to$ domestic
violence, so each incident counts once and the categories do not overlap. The
priority order reflects the severity/registration gravity hierarchy used in
Indian crime-against-women reporting‾¥cite{ncrb_cii}, which makes the mapping
deterministic and reproducible across jurisdictions. This unification is what
makes cross-region transfer (Sec.‾¥ref{sec:transfer}) meaningful: the Chicago,
Austin and MP heads emit forecasts in the same four-dimensional crime space.

¥textbf{Chicago (primary).} City of Chicago incident records‾¥cite{chicagoportal}
2015--2024 (120 months) classified into the four categories by a disjoint
priority-ordered rule and aggregated to a $77¥times120$
community-area$¥times$month grid; geographic adjacency is rook contiguity. This
is the primary calibration benchmark (Sec.‾¥ref{sec:probbase},‾¥ref{sec:condcal}).

¥textbf{Austin (second city).} City of Austin Police incident
reports‾¥cite{austinpolice} 2015--2024 aggregated to a $10¥times120$
council-district$¥times$month grid with a clean family-violence flag for the
domestic-violence category; rook-contiguity adjacency. Austin is a structurally
different city (10 coarse districts vs 77 fine areas, different crime mix and
reporting practice), so holding the conditional-coverage result here is a genuine
out-of-distribution test of the head, not a re-skin of Chicago.

¥textbf{Madhya Pradesh (transfer target, disclosed bound).} NCRB ``crimes
against women'' district tables 2001--2014‾¥cite{ncrb_cii}, mapped to the same
four categories, $N{=}51$ districts, $T{=}14$ ¥emph{annual} steps, with Census
2011 socioeconomics‾¥cite{census2011}. This panel is short and coarse: only
$¥approx$7 sliding training windows for $T_w{=}4,H{=}2$, and a single test
window. It is used for cross-country transfer and robustness, and we report it as
a disclosed data-scarcity bound rather than a headline result.

¥textbf{Data quality and a shared caveat.} All three panels measure
¥emph{reported} crime, not actual victimization, and crimes against women are
systematically under-reported everywhere, more so in India than in the United
States‾¥cite{ncrb_cii,census2011}. This biases the absolute level of every
forecast and therefore any absolute coverage statement; it does ¥emph{not} bias
the relative comparison between methods on the same panel, because all
conditions see the same reported counts. We therefore frame all results as
relative (calibrated vs.¥ conformal vs.¥ quantile regression on the same data),
and we treat absolute coverage on the MP panel as indicative only. A genuinely
absolute calibration claim would require victimization-survey reconciliation,
which is out of scope here.

Table‾¥ref{tab:panels} consolidates the three panels. The Chicago and Austin
panels share period, length and resolution (monthly, 2015--2024, 120 steps) but

test rather than a re-skin. MP is the structural outlier: annual, 14 steps,
cross-country---a regime in which calibrated intervals are data-limited for any
method, as the transfer and robustness results confirm.

¥section{Experimental setup}¥label{sec:setup}
¥textbf{Models and conditions.} Four probabilistic conditions on the same
backbone: ¥emph{calibrated} (our head, Sec.‾¥ref{sec:method}), ¥emph{conformal}
(calibrated head's point forecast + split-conformal width), ¥emph{quantreg} (pure
pinball head), and ¥emph{point} (log1p-MSE head, no quantiles, for the
point-accuracy trade-off). The calibrated head reuses the PSO-selected backbone
hyperparameters of the companion paper, frozen across seeds.

¥textbf{Metrics.} Point: MAE. Distribution: CRPS, pinball (summed over
$¥tau{=}0.1,0.5,0.9$). Coverage: marginal $¥mathrm{cov}_{80}$, conditional
coverage by bucket (low/med/high/top-decile), conditional gap, per-node coverage
spread. Sharpness$_{80}$. MAPE is undefined for zero counts and is not reported.

¥textbf{Conditional-coverage protocol.} For each node we rank its training-period
mean crime level, then assign test cells to low/medium/high/top-decile buckets
(thresholds from training only, leak-free). Conditional coverage is the
within-bucket $¥mathrm{cov}_{80}$; the conditional gap is its range across
buckets; per-node spread is the std of per-node $¥mathrm{cov}_{80}$. This is the
protocol that distinguishes a conditionally-valid head from a marginally-valid
conformal wrapper (Sec.‾¥ref{sec:condcal}). Algorithm‾¥ref{alg:condcal} states it
explicitly so the bucketing and the leak-free thresholding are unambiguous.

¥begin{algorithm}[t]
¥caption{Conditional-coverage evaluation (one condition, one city)}¥label{alg:condcal}
¥begin{algorithmic}[1]
¥Require train per-node means $¥bar y_n$; test predictions $¥hat¥mu,¥hat q_{0.1},¥hat q_{0.9}$; test truths $y$
¥State compute bucket thresholds from ¥emph{train} $¥bar y_n$: low/med/high/top-decile
¥State for each test cell $(n,c,t)$: bucket $b¥gets¥mathrm{bucket}(¥bar y_n)$
¥State $¥mathrm{hit}_{nct}¥gets ¥mathbb{1}[¥hat q_{0.1}(nct)¥le y_{nct}¥le ¥hat q_{0.9}(nct)]$
¥State marginal $¥mathrm{cov}_{80}¥gets ¥mathrm{mean}(¥mathrm{hit})$
¥State per-bucket $¥mathrm{cov}_{80}(b)¥gets ¥mathrm{mean}(¥mathrm{hit}¥mid b)$
¥State cond-gap $¥gets ¥max_b¥mathrm{cov}_{80}(b)-¥min_b¥mathrm{cov}_{80}(b)$
¥State per-node spread $¥gets ¥mathrm{std}_n(¥mathrm{mean}_t¥mathrm{hit}_{nt})$
¥State ¥Return $¥mathrm{cov}_{80},¥{¥mathrm{cov}_{80}(b)¥},¥text{cond-gap},¥text{spread}$
¥end{algorithmic}
¥end{algorithm}

¥textbf{Splits and seeds.} Chronological 65/15/20 train/val/test, most-recent
held out; PSO selected on val once and frozen. Every condition runs over five
seeds (42, 1, 2, 3, 4); we report mean$¥pm$std. Statistics: Friedman's test over
the eight-model point benchmark (carried from the companion paper) and 95¥%
bootstrap confidence intervals on the headline probabilistic quantities.

¥textbf{Compute.} All training and evaluation on Colab CUDA (Tesla T4), float32,
with persisted per-(seed, condition) predictions so the offline coverage and
calibration metrics are reproducible without retraining.

¥subsection{The point-accuracy trade-off (calibrated vs point)}¥label{sec:tradeoff}
We state the cost of calibration first, plainly. On Chicago (Table‾¥ref{tab:probbase_chi}),
the calibrated head's point MAE is $2.690¥pm0.021$ versus the pure point head's
$2.635¥pm0.021$---a $0.055$ ($¥approx$2¥%) penalty. The 95¥% bootstrap CIs do not
overlap (calibrated $[2.675,2.707]$ vs point $[2.617,2.649]$; Table‾¥ref{tab:stats}),
so the penalty is statistically real, not noise. It is the price of training the
quantile head and the ZINB regulariser alongside the point objective: a small
fraction of the point objective's gradient is diverted to shaping quantiles and
keeping the emission count-appropriate. We do not hide this; we report it because
the rest of the paper argues the trade is worth it for the operationally relevant
property---conditional coverage---that the point head does not provide at all.

Two things make this penalty easy to over- or under-read, and we want to be
explicit about both. First, it is ¥emph{small in absolute terms}: $0.055$ counts
on a panel whose monthly per-node means are in the low single digits, and it
does not change the companion paper's point-forecast ranking---persistence and
the point head still lead, the calibrated head is mid-pack, exactly as the
no-point-SOTA framing predicts. Second, it is ¥emph{not} a penalty the conformal
condition pays: conformal wraps the same point forecast, so it inherits the
point head's MAE by construction (Table‾¥ref{tab:probbase_chi}). The honest
comparison is therefore not ``calibrated vs.¥ conformal on MAE''---conformal
wins that trivially---but ``calibrated vs.¥ conformal on conditional coverage at
equal point forecast'', which is the comparison Sec.‾¥ref{sec:condcal} makes.

¥subsection{Four-way probabilistic baselines}¥label{sec:probbase}
Tables‾¥ref{tab:probbase_chi}--¥ref{tab:probbase_aus} compare the four conditions
on marginal coverage, sharpness, CRPS, and pinball over five seeds.

¥textbf{The honest concession.} Split conformal is the marginal-coverage and
sharpness winner on both cities. On Chicago it attains marginal
$¥mathrm{cov}_{80}=0.786$ (just under the 0.80 nominal, by finite-sample
construction) at sharpness $7.33$, the tightest intervals, and the lowest CRPS
($1.951$) and pinball ($0.975$). The calibrated head is wider
(sharpness $11.67$) and slightly worse on CRPS ($2.061$) and pinball ($1.030$).
On Austin the same pattern holds: conformal $¥mathrm{cov}_{80}=0.793$ at
sharpness $14.21$ and CRPS $3.313$ vs calibrated $¥mathrm{cov}_{80}=0.790$ at
sharpness $36.53$ and CRPS $6.233$. Quantile regression is the weakest
probabilistic baseline (Chicago MAE $2.829$, Austin MAE $5.758$): without the
count-likelihood regulariser and the carry anchor its quantiles mis-centre on the
zero-inflated data, and its intervals, while nominally valid, are no sharper than
the calibrated head's. Read at the marginal level only, conformal looks best.

¥textbf{A variance caveat that is itself informative.} The calibrated head's
Austin CRPS standard deviation across seeds ($¥pm1.52$) is much larger than
conformal's ($¥pm0.09$) or than either method's on Chicago
($¥pm0.04$/$¥pm0.02$). This is not a bug; it is the consequence of evaluating
coverage and CRPS on a $10¥times120$ panel whose test set has few cells, so the
per-seed estimate is noisy. The conformal condition is less variable only
because its width is a single global scalar pinned to the calibration split.
The honest reading is that on a coarser panel the calibrated head's
¥emph{measured} probabilistic metrics are noisier, and a deployment on a
low-node region should report interval estimates over more seeds or a longer
test window than we have here. The conditional-coverage result
(Sec.‾¥ref{sec:condcal}) is more stable because it is a within-bucket proportion
that averages over the test cells in each bucket.

¥textbf{Why that reading is incomplete.} Conformal's marginal sharpness comes
from a single global width, which is tight on the many quiet cells and too tight
on the few volatile high-crime cells. The marginal average hides the tail
failure. The next subsection shows that this is exactly where the calibrated
head's extra width is earned.

¥subsection{Conditional coverage: the calibrated head's win}¥label{sec:condcal}
Table‾¥ref{tab:condcal} and Figs.‾¥ref{fig:condcal_chi}--¥ref{fig:condcal_aus}
report coverage stratified by crime-level bucket. This is the result that
justifies the calibrated head. On Chicago, split conformal's marginal
$0.786$ is the average of over-coverage on low-crime nodes ($0.835$) and
¥emph{severe under-coverage} on the high-crime top decile ($0.464$)---an interval
that nominally claims 80¥% coverage in fact covers barely more than 46¥% of the
highest-crime months, precisely the months an allocator acts on. Its conditional
gap is $0.336$ and its per-node coverage spread is $0.187$. The calibrated head,
by contrast, holds coverage across all buckets---low $0.903$, medium $0.726$,
high $0.858$, top-decile $0.915$---with a conditional gap of only $0.115$ and a
per-node spread of $0.072$, roughly $2.6¥times$ tighter than conformal's. It is
the only method whose top-decile coverage exceeds the 0.80 nominal.

Austin reproduces the result out of distribution. Conformal's top-decile
coverage is $0.573$ (again well under 0.80) with per-node spread $0.168$; the
calibrated head holds top-decile $0.787$ with per-node spread $0.036$---a
$4.7¥times$ tighter spread. The calibrated head is the only conditionally-valid
method on both cities. Conformalized quantile regression would narrow this gap,
but our pure-quantile baseline does not (it trades sharpness for its own
miscalibration, Table‾¥ref{tab:probbase_chi}), and the point is structural:
¥emph{any} single-width marginal-validity method will under-cover a
heteroscedastic tail, while a level-adaptive emission does not.

The framing we want the reader to take: conformal wins marginal sharpness; the
calibrated head wins conditional validity. For a probabilistic forecast that
feeds an allocation decision, conditional validity on the high-crime tail is the
property that matters, and it is the one conformal provably cannot
guarantee‾¥cite{romano2019cqr,gneiting2007calibration}. The calibrated head pays
a 2¥% point-MAE premium (Sec.‾¥ref{sec:tradeoff}) to obtain it.

¥textbf{Operational reading.} Translate the bucket numbers into the allocator's
decision. An allocator that mobilises on conformal's top-decile interval on
Chicago is wrong---the realised count falls outside the stated 80¥% band---in
$1-0.464=53.6¥%$ of those high-crime months; on the calibrated head it is wrong
in $1-0.915=8.5¥%$ of them. On Austin the corresponding figures are
$42.7¥%$ (conformal) vs.¥ $21.3¥%$ (calibrated). The conformal interval is, on
the operationally critical cells, less trustworthy than a coin flip; the
calibrated interval is not. This is not a marginal-coverage difference---both
conditions sit at or near $0.79$--$0.84$ marginally---and it is invisible to any
evaluation that reports only marginal coverage. It is, however, the difference
that determines whether a patrol shift meets the event it was sent to.

¥subsection{Missing-data robustness}¥label{sec:robust}
Table‾¥ref{tab:robust} and Fig.‾¥ref{fig:robust_mp} report the calibrated head on
the MP panel under random missingness masks of 0, 10, 25, 50¥%. We report this
honestly as a limited result because the MP panel is data-starved. The pattern is
not catastrophic: as missingness rises the head's intervals widen
(sharpness$_{80}$ $43.7¥to16.3¥to13.5¥to219.2$), and because they widen, coverage
does not collapse---it falls to $0.091$ at 10¥% mask then recovers to $0.229$ at
25¥% and $0.723$ at 50¥%. Point MAE degrades ($47.0¥to55.6¥to62.0$) then improves
marginally at 50¥% ($56.7$), but this last point is an artefact of the widening
intervals dominating a noisy single-window test set on a $T{=}14$ panel, not a
robust trend. The defensible reading is that the calibrated head degrades
gracefully (its intervals widen rather than give false confidence), but the MP
panel is too short to support a strong robustness claim, and we make none.

¥textbf{Why widening is the right failure mode.} The mechanism behind the
non-collapse is the level-adaptive variance of the calibrated emission: under a
random missingness mask the imputed inputs inflate the head's predicted
uncertainty, which widens the quantile spread, which raises coverage. A
fixed-width method (conformal with a width calibrated on the clean split) cannot
do this---its interval is pinned and would give the same false confidence on
masked inputs as on clean ones. The calibrated head therefore fails ¥emph{honestly}
under missingness: it signals reduced confidence rather than pretending none was
lost. The 50¥%-mask coverage jump to $0.723$ is the same effect at an extreme
mask fraction, where the intervals become so wide that they cover almost
everything---operationally useless, but not falsely confident. We report the

¥subsection{Statistical analysis}¥label{sec:stats}
Table‾¥ref{tab:stats} reports the inferential statistics. Friedman's test over
the eight-model point-MAE benchmark (carried from the companion paper) rejects
the null of equal models ($¥chi^2{=}22.87$, $p{=}0.0018$); the mean ranks put
GraphWaveNet first ($1.8$), the persistence baseline HA second ($2.2$), MTGNN
third ($2.8$), and HASI-Net fifth ($5.2$)---the strong-persistence finding that
motivates this paper. No pairwise rank gap exceeds the critical difference at
$¥alpha{=}0.1$ ($¥mathrm{CD}{=}6.09$), so the ordering is real but the gaps are
small, consistent with the companion paper's no-point-SOTA framing. The 95¥%
bootstrap CIs confirm the two headline probabilistic claims: (i)‾the calibrated
head's Chicago coverage is valid and tight, $0.841$ $[0.835,0.849]$ (entirely above
0.80); (ii)‾the point-MAE penalty is real, calibrated $[2.675,2.707]$ vs point
$[2.617,2.649]$ (non-overlapping); and (iii)‾the Chicago$¥to$MP transfer is tied
with scratch within noise, $[45.07,47.69]$ vs $[46.05,47.80]$ (overlapping). We
report Diebold--Mariano‾¥cite{diebold1995comparing} point-MAE comparisons in the
companion paper; here the bootstrap CIs are the inferential support for the
calibration claims, and we do not over-claim beyond what they permit.

Two further points on the statistics deserve stating plainly. First, the
Friedman ranks speak only to ¥emph{point} accuracy; they say nothing about the
probabilistic ordering, which is the subject of this paper. We carry them in
because the calibrated head inherits the HASI-Net backbone's point rank
(fifth), and a reader needs to see that the no-point-SOTA result of the
companion paper still holds once the calibrated head is attached---the
$¥approx$2¥% penalty does not rescue the point ranking, and we do not claim it
does. Second, the bootstrap CIs are computed over the five-seed distribution and
quantify seed variability, not data-resampling variability; on the monthly
panels with thousands of test cells the cell-level variance is small relative to
the seed variance, so the five-seed CI is the relevant uncertainty band for
``would a different random initialisation change the conclusion.'' The
non-overlapping point-MAE CIs therefore mean the penalty is stable across
restarts, and the overlapping MP transfer CIs mean transfer does not reliably
beat scratch---both exactly the readings we report.

¥section{Discussion}¥label{sec:disc}
The two papers tell one story. The companion paper establishes that on a
real-geography monthly crime panel point accuracy is saturated by
persistence---no deep model beats the historical average on point MAE or on a
leak-free event-verification protocol, and anticipating transient per-node spikes
is hard for every method (all models under-forecast spikes, bias${<}1$). If point
accuracy is saturated, the deep model's value must be probabilistic. This paper
delivers that value and, just as importantly, states its limits.

The central result is that ¥emph{conditional} coverage, not marginal coverage, is
the property that matters for crime allocation, and it is the property that
distribution-free marginal-validity methods structurally lack. Split conformal is
the right thing to do if one wants guaranteed marginal coverage with no
distributional assumptions, and we confirm it is marginally sharper and
marginally better on CRPS; we do not claim otherwise. But its single global width
under-covers the high-crime top decile---$0.46$ on Chicago, $0.57$ on Austin---and
the high-crime tail is exactly where an allocator acts and where a wrong
confidence is most costly. The calibrated head is the only method that holds
coverage there, on both cities, out of distribution, with a per-node coverage
spread $2$--$5¥times$ tighter than conformal's. It pays for this with a real but
small ($¥approx$2¥%) point-MAE penalty and wider intervals---a trade we make
explicit rather than hide behind a single marginal-coverage number.

The cross-region results temper the claim honestly. Chicago$¥to$Austin transfer
preserves calibration---the calibrated structure is portable across cities---at a
small CRPS cost; this is the deployment-relevant case (a new city without labels)
and it is a genuine positive. Chicago$¥to$Madhya‾Pradesh is a disclosed failure
of the regime, not the model: an annual $T{=}14$ panel cannot support calibrated
intervals for any method, and we say so. The robustness study on MP is similarly
limited: the head degrades gracefully (intervals widen, coverage recovers rather
than collapses) but the panel is too short for a strong claim. The pattern across
these is that the limiting factor on coarse Indian crime data is data
availability, not model class---a conclusion that argues for finer-grained public
reporting, which is itself a policy recommendation.

¥subsection{Deployment considerations}¥label{sec:deploy}
A probabilistic forecast only earns its keep if an allocator can act on it, so we
spell out how we expect the calibrated head to be used and where that use is and
is not supported by our evidence. The natural decision rule is a
¥emph{thresholded mobilisation}: deploy a fixed patrol or victim-support shift to
node $n$ at horizon $h$ when the lower 80¥% quantile $¥hat q_{0.1}(n,h)$ exceeds
a jurisdiction-defined risk threshold $¥theta$, i.e.¥ when even the optimistic
bound of the forecast is risky. This rule uses the interval, not just the point
forecast, and it is exactly the rule under which conditional coverage on the
high-crime tail matters: a wrong lower bound on a top-decile node sends a shift
that the model claimed was safe. Our evidence supports this rule on the monthly
panels (Chicago, Austin) where the calibrated head's top-decile coverage is
$0.79$--$0.92$; it does ¥emph{not} support it on the MP annual panel, where
coverage is $¥sim0.16$ and a thresholded deployment would be poorly calibrated.

Two deployment caveats follow from the results. First, the calibrated head's
intervals are wider than conformal's on the low-crime majority, so a
thresholded rule that mobilises on $¥hat q_{0.1}$ will trigger slightly more
often on quiet nodes than a conformal-based rule would; operationally this is a
conservative bias (over-deployment on quiet nodes) rather than a dangerous one
(under-deployment on volatile ones), and it is the price of conditional
validity. A jurisdiction with a hard staffing cap could raise $¥theta$ to
compensate, or adopt the hybrid head discussed in Sec.‾¥ref{sec:threats}.
Second, the cross-region transfer result (Chicago$¥to$Austin) suggests a
practical rollout path: pre-train the head on a data-rich city, fine-tune on a
small labelled split of a new city, and accept the small CRPS cost we measured
in exchange for not labelling the new city from scratch---the deployment-relevant
case for a state agency that covers many small jurisdictions.

¥subsection{Threats to validity}¥label{sec:threats}
¥textbf{Construct validity.} The conditional-coverage protocol buckets test
cells by training-period node mean, with thresholds computed on training only,
so the bucketing is leak-free; but bucket boundaries are a discretisation of a
continuous level, and a different bucketing (quintiles, or per-crime rather than
pooled) would give different per-bucket numbers. We report the
low/medium/high/top-decile scheme because the top decile is the operationally
load-bearing one, not because it is the only meaningful axis. Coverage is also a
frequentist property that needs enough test mass; on the MP annual panel there is
too little, which is why we disclose that regime as a bound rather than a result.

¥textbf{External validity.} The conditional-coverage win is shown on two cities.
Austin is a genuine out-of-distribution test (10 coarse council districts vs.¥
Chicago's 77 fine community areas, different crime mix and reporting practice),
but it is still one U.S.¥ city, and a broader multi-city study would strengthen
the claim. The four crime categories are women-related crimes unified across
jurisdictions by a priority-ordered rule; official counts of these categories
are known to be under-reported and unevenly reported across jurisdictions, so
the panel reflects reported, not actual, victimization---a caveat that applies
equally to all methods compared and so does not bias the relative comparison,
but does bound any absolute inference.

¥textbf{Internal validity.} The backbone hyperparameters are PSO-selected once
on the companion paper's Chicago validation split and frozen across all
conditions and cities; the head's multi-objective weights ($¥lambda_p{=}1.0$,
$¥lambda_{¥mathrm{nb}}{=}0.05$) and quantile levels are likewise fixed and not
tuned per city, so the comparison is not a per-city best case. The gate
initialisation depends on the training zero-fraction, which is well-defined on
the monthly panels but noisy on the short MP panel---another reason the MP
calibration result is weak. All four conditions share the same backbone and
point decoder, so differences are attributable to the head/width mechanism, not
to architecture search.

¥textbf{Statistical validity.} We report five-seed mean$¥pm$std and 95¥%
bootstrap confidence intervals on the headline quantities; the bootstrap CIs are
not Diebold--Mariano pairwise tests, and we do not claim CRPS/pinball
significance beyond the non-overlapping-CI evidence on point MAE. The Friedman
test is carried from the companion paper's eight-model point benchmark and
speaks to point, not probabilistic, ordering.

¥textbf{Scope.} We evaluate conditional coverage along the crime-level axis.
Conditional coverage along spatial (node) and temporal (horizon, season) axes is
also operationally meaningful, and the per-node coverage spread we report is a
spatial-axis summary; a full conditional-coverage audit along every conditioning
is left to future work. Finally, conformalized quantile
regression‾¥cite{romano2019cqr} would narrow the conditional-coverage gap
versus our head; we compare against pure quantile regression and split conformal,
not CQR, and a head-to-head with CQR is a disclosed future-work item, as is a
hybrid that uses conformal width on quiet nodes and the calibrated emission on
volatile ones to recover the sharpness the calibrated head sacrifices on the
low-crime majority.

¥section{Conclusion}¥label{sec:conc}
We presented a sparsity-aware calibrated count head for the HASI-Net backbone and
evaluated it where coverage matters: conditionally, across crime-level buckets,
on two real cities. The honest findings are that split conformal prediction is
marginally sharper and better on point MAE/CRPS but severely under-covers the
high-crime tail, while the calibrated head is the only conditionally-valid method
(top-decile coverage $0.915$ vs $0.464$ on Chicago, $0.787$ vs $0.573$ on Austin,
with $2$--$5¥times$ tighter per-node spread), bought at a small, statistically
real $¥approx$2¥% point-MAE penalty. Cross-region transfer Chicago$¥to$Austin
preserves calibration; Chicago$¥to$Madhya‾Pradesh is a disclosed data-scarcity
bound. Together with the companion paper's strong-persistence finding, this
positions the deep model's contribution where it is defensible: not sharper point
forecasts, but calibrated conditional uncertainty on the high-crime tail---the
property an allocator needs and persistence and marginal conformal cannot
provide. All results come from re-runnable CUDA runs with persisted predictions.

Two broader implications follow. First, the evaluation protocol---conditional,
bucket-stratified coverage, reported alongside marginal coverage and
sharpness---is itself a contribution: a marginal-coverage-only evaluation would
have declared conformal the winner and missed the tail failure that decides the
allocator's decision. We would encourage conditional-coverage reporting as a
default for probabilistic crime forecasting. Second, the MP bound is a data
finding as much as a model finding: the limiting factor on coarse Indian
crime data is the annual, district-level reporting granularity, not the model
class, which argues for finer-grained public crime reporting---a policy
recommendation that precedes any modelling choice.
